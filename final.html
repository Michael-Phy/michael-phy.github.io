Final Project: Grip-Assist Tableware Holder for Parkinson’s Patients

Project Background & Purpose

I am a high school student in Harvard’s Secondary School Summer Program. I like physics and engineering very much. This light arm holder helps people with Parkinson’s disease. These people have weak hand strength and shaky hands. They cannot hold chopsticks or spoons steadily, so it is hard for them to eat alone. This cheap self-made tool uses soft plastic parts to reduce shaking. It also has an electric lock to hold tableware tight when hands lose strength.

Three Required Skills I Will Use in This Class

1. 3D Design & Digital Fabrication

I will draw all parts of the arm holder in Fusion 360. I will make two different versions to test the best shape. I use two kinds of plastic for 3D printing: hard PLA for the main frame, and soft TPU to soften shaking and avoid hurting skin. The frame uses small magnets to connect pieces together, no glue needed. There are empty spaces inside the frame to put the motor and sensor parts.

2. Electronics Input & Output System

Input Sensor: A thin pressure sensor to check how hard users hold their chopsticks or spoons.
Output Parts: A small motor to lock tableware automatically; LED lights controlled by a light sensor for dark places; a tiny buzzer to make short sounds when users hold too weakly.

3. Microcontroller Programming (Arduino)

Arduino reads numbers from the pressure sensor all the time. If the holding force is too low, the motor turns to clamp the tableware, and the buzzer makes a short sound to remind the user. When the room is dark, LED lights turn on by themselves. All control rules are simple number checks, no hard math calculations.

How This Device Works

The device has two main working parts: soft plastic shock reduction and simple electric control.
1. Soft TPU plastic inside the arm frame absorbs hand shaking. It stops big shakes from moving to chopsticks.
2. The pressure sensor feels the user’s grip strength. When hands become weak, the motor locks the tableware tightly, and the buzzer reminds the user.
3. A light sensor checks brightness. It turns on LED lights when the dining area is dark.

Production Timeline

  • Week 2–3: Measure arm size, draw 3D models, print and improve the arm holder
  • Week 4–6: Connect all electronic wires and put parts into the printed frame
  • Week 7–9: Write simple Arduino code and set the right sensor numbers
  • Week 10–14: Test the device while using it, fix small problems, record demo videos for my website

Why This Project Is Special

Most student projects only grab small objects. This wearable tool helps sick people finish daily meals. It uses soft plastic to cut down shaking and easy electric parts to assist weak hands. It solves real daily life problems for people with hand tremors.
